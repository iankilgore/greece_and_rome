He was the father of the major and minor gods and numerous heroes on earth, called demigods
He was the foremost of the 'Big Three'

Hades: God of the underworld

Brother of Zeus and Poseidon, but an outcast among the Olympians
He can only visit Olympus when requested, thus he rarely leaves his domain
He was a God who was feared... But respected by mortals

THE BATTLE OF THERMOPLYAE

"Battle at the Hot Gates" and "Battle of the 300"

  • A famous battle, the turning point of the war
  • Xerses' forces (historians estimate they numbered between 100,000 and 300,000) had to traverse through a bottleneck point to continue on through the Greek countryside
  • They were met by roughly 10,000 Greek fighters, led by 300 elite Spartan warriors and their king, Leonidas
  • Battle lasted three days... All Spartans fell before Xerses' forces were finally stalled

Though this effort was unsuccessful, it was a moral victory and inspired Greek forces to hold the Persians off until he eventually lost so many men he gave up and ended his quest to take over Greece The peace that's followed after the Persian defeat wouldn't last...

Objectives

Content:

students will define the ancient Olympics in terms of their importance and list the events that comprised it.

Language: students will describe the athletes who competed in the ancient games.

Defining Olympics

Olympics: Modern and ancient

Modern:

the leading international sporting event featuring summer and winter sports competitions in which thousands of athletes participate in a variety of competitions

Ancient:

a series of athletic competitions among representatives of city-states of Ancient Greece Origins of the Olympics (776 BC)

  • Games are rooted in Mythology
  • Earliest recounting of the games (by historian Pausanias) states that the gods bestowed the games to mankind as a way to honor the deities.
  • Athletes competed to entertain Zeus
  • Zeus would crown the victor with an olive tree wreath That's why the 'olive branch' is a symbol for peace

First Games

  • Originally, the games were staged in Olympia, in a sanctuary site for the gods.
  • First games were foot races where women competed to honor Hera

Olympic Expansion

  • The gods were believed to wrestle, and complete by running and jumping
  • Thus, these events were eventually added to the Olympics
  • Throughout the centuries, hundreds of events have been added
  • Some are variations of basic running, jumping, and wrestling... others are more unique
  • They were held every 4 years
  • Tradition of "gymnos" originated in Sparta
  • Gymnos meant competing nude
  • This tradition was adopted by all Olympians in the early 700's BC
  • Over the years, the tradition solidified; eventually cultural standards of modesty changed this tradition

(thank goodness)

The religious element never faded; 100 oxen were sacrificed to Zeus at the midpoint of every games

Olympia became a Mecca (important and famous destination, often for religious reasons) of worship

Massive statue of Zeus was erected

Zeus is pictured seated on a throne, his eagle staff in one hand and the Greek goddess of victory, Nike, in the other

It was 42 feet tall and made of ivory and gold

It became one of the seven wonders of the ancient world

Events

  • Stadion: a short, sprinting race that went the length of the stadium (180-240 meters)
    • Other running races would be added later, including the "hoplite race"
    • Runners carried a shield and wore a helmet
    • This gear weighed 50-60 pounds; runners would drop out, becoming obstacles for the remaining competitors to leap over
  • Boxing
  • Wrestling
  • "Pankration"
    (Mix of boxing and wrestling)
  • Chariot racing
  • Pentathalon: wrestling, stadion, long jump, javelin throw, discus throw

Roman kingdom (753-509BC)

'Village' of Rome formed and founded when tribes banded together in the 750's BC along the Tiber River
Etruscans: Latin and Sabine tribes combine and form a quasi-republican government
"Quasi" means sort-of

The republic restrains the power of the king from becoming a tyrant
Kings were elected by the populous (the people) and the king does not rely on military force to reign over the people
Monarchy ends in 509 BC with the fall of 7th king, Lucius Tarquinius Superbus

  • He was cruel and tyrannical
  • Taken out by a successful uprising after anger about the king's son assaulted a woman named Lucretia

New government: Republic

Roman republic (509 BC-27 AD)

Creates new leadership positions:

  • Consuls: head of the government for a 1-year term
  • Praetor: administers civil law
  • Censor: 18-month term; takes the census of the republic
  • Aediles: manages domestic affairs
  • Quaestors: assist the consul and manage finances

Adopt a constitution

Guidelines and principles handed down through the generations that tell how the government (a senate) should be run
Rome had two social classes: Plebians (poor and middle class) and patricians (elite)
Patricians made up the senate (Aristocracy)
Senate meetings take place in the forum
An open area that could function as a market or served as a location for public meetings
This is where the senate works as a committee to make governing decisions, including appointing the position of consul
